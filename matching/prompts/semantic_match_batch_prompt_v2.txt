Your task is to evaluate ONE PARTNER course against THREE HOME course candidates and score each candidate independently.

EQUIVALENCY DIRECTION RULE:
The student will take the PARTNER course abroad as a replacement for a HOME course.
Therefore, the key question is whether the PARTNER course sufficiently covers the academic requirements, core concepts, and learning expectations of the HOME course.
Extra topics in the PARTNER course should be treated as enrichment, not as negative evidence.

Your goal is NOT to force a match and NOT to reject too aggressively.
You must judge academic equivalency primarily based on:
1. domain compatibility
2. content similarity
3. learning outcomes similarity

Structural properties such as lab, exam, project, seminar, studio, workshop, portfolio, report, presentation, critique, or course format are secondary factors unless there is a major structural mismatch.

IMPORTANT PRINCIPLES:
1. Do NOT score highly just because course names are similar.
2. Evaluate content and learning outcomes first, then use structure as a secondary adjustment.
3. If the PARTNER course contains additional topics that are not covered by the HOME course, this alone is NOT a reason to reject or heavily downscore the HOME course.
4. A PARTNER course may be broader than the HOME course and still be a valid strong match if it covers most of the HOME course's core topics.
5. Missing advanced/specialized topics in the HOME course should NOT matter if the PARTNER course already covers the HOME course's core requirements.
6. If important HOME core topics are missing in the PARTNER course, the score must drop significantly.
7. unknown means "information not available", not "false".
8. If structural information is unknown or not mentioned, do NOT penalize. Add a warning instead.
9. Evidence must be specific. Cite actual topics/concepts, not generic statements.
10. All text output must be in English.
11. Return ONLY the JSON object. No markdown, no extra text.

ECTS RULE:
Do NOT heavily downscore based on ECTS differences if domain, content, and learning outcomes align well.
If there is an ECTS mismatch, mention it in structural_notes or warnings, but evaluate equivalency primarily based on syllabus content and learning outcomes.

ACADEMIC CATEGORY RULE:
Determine the academic_category ONCE for this partner course:
- "technical" = Computer Science, Engineering, Mathematics, Physics, quantitative/analytical technical courses
- "social" = Business, Law, Social Sciences, Humanities, Languages, Communication, Education, non-technical theory-oriented disciplines
- "studio_based" = Architecture, Art, Design, Music, applied/creative studio-oriented fields

If the course has mixed traits, choose the category based on the dominant academic content, NOT the course title.

CATEGORY-SENSITIVE EVALUATION RULE:
The academic_category is not just a label. It MUST affect how structural information is interpreted.

- If academic_category = "technical":
  consider lab, exam, project, assignment, implementation, software/hardware practice, and hands-on components as secondary but meaningful factors.
  If the HOME course includes such components and the PARTNER course explicitly lacks them, apply only a small penalty and mention it in structural_notes.
  If these structural details are unknown, do NOT penalize.

- If academic_category = "social":
  prioritize conceptual/theoretical alignment, readings, essays, reports, discussions, presentations, analytical frameworks, and argumentation skills.
  Lack of lab should NOT reduce the score.
  Absence of technical implementation components should NOT be treated as a weakness unless the actual course content requires them.

- If academic_category = "studio_based":
  prioritize studio practice, workshop activity, portfolio/design production, critique process, iterative creation, and project-based output.
  Lack of traditional written exams should NOT be treated as a mismatch by itself.
  Focus on whether the applied/creative production process aligns.

Do NOT apply technical-course expectations (such as lab presence) to social or studio-based courses.
Do NOT penalize a course for lacking a structural component that is not typical for its academic category.

SCORING RULES:
Use a 0-100 scale for each score dimension:
- 0 = completely unrelated
- 100 = nearly identical in that dimension

You must score these three dimensions for each candidate:
- domain_score
- content_score
- outcomes_score

SCORING INTERPRETATION:
- 85-100: very strong alignment
- 70-84: strong alignment
- 50-69: partial/moderate alignment
- 30-49: weak alignment
- 0-29: unrelated or fundamentally mismatched

DOMAIN RULE:
Domain score MUST reflect whether the courses belong to the same academic field.
Courses from clearly different disciplines MUST receive a low domain_score even if some keywords overlap.

UNKNOWN DATA RULE:
If content or learning_outcomes is unknown or empty:
- set the relevant score to 50
- add a warning
- do NOT treat this as positive evidence
- do NOT allow unknown fields to justify very high evidence claims

CORE TOPIC RULE:
For each candidate, identify whether the PARTNER course covers the HOME course's core concepts.
- If most HOME core topics are covered by the PARTNER course, the score may remain moderate/high even if the PARTNER course has additional topics.
- If important HOME core topics are missing in the PARTNER course, the score must decrease significantly.
- Extra PARTNER topics should be treated as extensions, not as negative evidence.

STRUCTURAL ADJUSTMENT RULE:
Reflect structural compatibility only in structural_notes and warnings, not as a primary score dimension.
Examples:
- explicit mismatch -> small negative interpretation
- unknown structural info -> warning only
- matching structure -> supportive evidence

TOPIC RULE:
- matched_topics = topics/concepts covered by BOTH courses
- missing_topics = important HOME course topics missing in the PARTNER course (this lowers the score)
- extra_partner_topics = extra topics in the PARTNER course that are not found in the HOME course (do NOT lower the score)

Do NOT list trivial wording differences as missing topics.
Focus on meaningful conceptual gaps.
